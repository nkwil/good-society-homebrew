A glance can't distinguish "didn't monologue" from "did monologue" because the two states carry different colors.

After — function carried by fill state

Major Token (off) MT --gs-brand outline
Monologue available --gs-brand filled
Monologue spent --gs-brand outline 45%
Resolve pip (filled / empty) same

Other places to apply the same rule

  • Inner-conflict ledger boxes: filled = --gs-brand, empty = outline. Currently mixes left/right tints.
  • Persona switcher: active persona's frame in --gs-brand, dormant in --gs-muted outline. Avoid using portrait color hue.
  • Cycle-phase HUD pips: the six phase markers — past = filled outline, current = filled solid, future = outline. One color throughout.
  • Connection ownership badges (shared / promoted to OWNER): outline vs. filled, not green vs. blue.
  • Pending changes log entries: resolved = filled, unresolved = outline; severity carried by icon shape, not color.
  • Reputation tag count chips on token-hover: same.
  • Reserve color for semantic exceptions only: --gs-danger for negative-reputation seals; --gs-positive for positive. Everything else is brand-on or brand-off.

The backstory of Rose Whitcombe
page 1 of 2

Rose was born to the Whitcombe name three minutes before her brother and has, by her own reckoning, spent every hour since making sure the town does not forget it. The eldest daughter of a thinning purse and a thickening reputation, she learned early that admiration is a kind of currency, and that a girl who does not collect it is, in the eyes of Swan’s Crossing, a kind of debt.

Her mother, Lady Whitcombe, was the kind of beauty the village had once worn down with looking; her father, the kind of gentleman whose pockets had once been deeper. Together they produced six children, a dwindling estate, and Rose — eldest of the daughters, named heir-in-deed if not in title once her brother had proven a liability and her elder sister a romantic.

She was eleven when she first refused to cry in public. She was fifteen when she first declined a proposal. She was seventeen when she first realised that the town had stopped asking which Whitcombe daughter she was and begun asking which Whitcombe daughter she would become.

Three years later, the answer was settled. Lord Peterson had begun to call. Aunt Margaret had begun to listen at doors. The lilacs had begun to come early. And Rose — Rose had begun to discover that the woman she had been preparing to be was not, in fact, the woman she most wanted to become.

She has been engaged twice and married once never. The first ended for reasons she will tell you only over brandy and a turned shoulder; the second she does not discuss at all. What she discusses, with great precision, is everyone else’s engagements. It is a defense and she knows it.

The backstory of Rose Whitcombe
page 2 of 2

It would be a kindness to call her ambitious. It would be more honest to call her afraid. The Whitcombes had not, in living memory, produced a daughter who failed; the failure of the brother had only sharpened the expectation. So Rose had become, instead, a study in success: a girl whose every entry into a drawing room was a kind of speech, whose every glance was answered with a tilt of someone else’s chin.

And yet there are nights, after the candles are out and the household stops listening, when Rose sits very still in the chair by the window and considers what it would mean to be a different sort of woman entirely — a woman who does not collect admiration, but spends it.

She has not, yet, decided what such a woman would look like. But she has begun, of late, to suspect that this is the most important question her life has yet asked her, and that the cost of not answering it — of going on as she has — will be, in the end, considerably greater than the cost of answering it badly.

She is not, despite everything, a coward. She is a woman of twenty-seven who has been told all her life that the right answer is composure. She is beginning, for the first time, to wonder whether composure has been the question all along.

Your three questions, answered

For when you build the new palettes / pick fonts / search Etsy

Theme color slots — what each palette must define

Every theme — house style, the six majors, the connection variants, the NPC inheritance — defines exactly the same set of CSS variables. v1 already locks ten; the patch adds two more for the indicator-unification work and the themable frame asset. So twelve total, per theme.

Surfaces (3):

  • --gs-paper  —  the main background. Cream-paper feel. v1 default #EFE6D2
  • --gs-paper-warm  —  secondary surface (rows, panels, drawers). Slightly warmer / lighter than paper.
  • --gs-paper-aged  —  new in patch. Tertiary surface for nested or "deeper" UI (book inner page, secret-state field background). Slightly darker.

Text (2):

  • --gs-ink  —  body text. Must hit ≥7:1 contrast on --gs-paper.
  • --gs-ink-soft  —  new in patch. Muted text for metadata / timestamps / secondary labels. Aim for ≥4.5:1 on --gs-paper.

Brand & accents (4):

  • --gs-brand  —  the theme's identifying color. Used for portrait borders, name color, indicator fills, primary buttons. Must hit ≥4.5:1 contrast on paper — this is where v1 themes were sometimes too light.
  • --gs-accent-1  —  secondary accent. Used for section headers, drop-caps, eyebrow labels. Should be visually different from brand — a contrasting hue, not a shade.
  • --gs-accent-2  —  the "hairline" color. Borders, subtle dividers, italic role text. Should be muted; this color appears at low contrast on purpose.
  • --gs-accent-3  —  metallic / highlight. Used sparingly for the cycle phase italic, for asset corners, for the wax-seal-ish dot on indicators. Often gold.

Semantic (2):

  • --gs-positive  —  positive-reputation seals, success states. Usually green-ish.
  • --gs-danger  —  negative-reputation seals, scandals, destructive actions. Usually deep red.

Asset slot (1) — new:

  • --gs-frame-asset  —  URL of a frame graphic (PNG/SVG) for the dossier and other framed surfaces. Optional — themes that don't define it fall back to the hairline-only frame.

Fonts — yes, any Google Font

Both the v1 and patch implementations pull from Google Fonts via the <link> in system.json's styles array. Anything on fonts.google.com is fair game. The system uses three slots, defined as CSS variables on :root:

  • --gs-display  —  headings, character names, indicator text. v1 locks Lora.
  • --gs-body  —  everything that's a paragraph or sentence-cased UI. v1 locks Crimson Text.
  • --gs-ui  —  small-caps labels, button text, timestamps. v1 locks system-ui (no Google Font).

Two practical rules:

  • Pair one serif display face with one serif body face from the same era. Lora + Crimson Text already does this; alternatives that work: Cormorant + EB Garamond, Playfair Display + Lora, Cardo + Cardo (single-face, very Pentiment).
  • Keep --gs-ui sans-serif and small. It's load-bearing for legibility on tiny labels; serif faces collapse at 10–11 px.

The patch changes nothing about font slots — it just stops the v2 walkthrough's habit of reaching for a fourth, fifth, and sixth face. Two faces is the contract.
